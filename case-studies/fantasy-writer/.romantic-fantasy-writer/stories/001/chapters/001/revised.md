# Chapter One

The seawall held. It always held. Kaelith counted the stones as she walked, checking mortar that did not need checking, doing work that was not work. Six years of this. Six years of walking a wall that would stand another century whether she touched it or not.

The Oathbrand flared.

She stopped. Her hand went to her left collarbone, fingers finding the ridge of scar tissue through her shirt. Heat spread from the brand in waves — sharp, then sharper, a familiar pain made suddenly wrong. The brand should have dissolved years ago. One year, they'd said. Standard disciplinary term. She'd counted each day of that year like stones in a wall, waiting for the burning to stop.

It never stopped.

She pressed harder. The pain spiked, then settled into its usual rhythm: a low burn that sharpened when she moved her shoulder, when she thought too hard, when she woke. Beneath the burn, for half a breath, something else — a pressure in her sternum, faint and deep, like a wall bearing weight on the other side. Then nothing. The flare passed. She lowered her hand.

The wall still held.

She resumed walking. Salt wind off the northeast rattled the loose boards someone had stacked against the garrison's outer curtain. Duskhaven smelled like brine and wet stone and the particular staleness of buildings maintained for no one. The fog had come in an hour before dawn and had not left. It never really left.

Overhead, the Pallor sat like tarnished silver. Permanent dusk. She'd stopped noticing it years ago, the way she'd stopped noticing the ceiling in the room where she slept.

The Pallor tore.

Kaelith looked up.

A seam opened in the silver membrane, narrow and bright. True starlight poured through — not the filtered grey she'd lived under her whole life, but light that had traveled uninterrupted from something burning impossibly far away. The tear was maybe ten feet across, ragged at the edges like stone stressed past its tolerance. She could see through it. Stars. Dozens of them. Cold and white and entirely too clear.

She counted three breaths. The tear sealed. The Pallor pressed closed, silver settling back into place like mortar finding a joint. The stars vanished.

Overhead: tarnished silver. Permanent dusk.

She stood on the seawall with one hand still pressed to her collarbone and tried to calculate what she'd just seen. The Pallor had been intact for seven hundred years. Intact meant functional. Functional meant the world stayed finished — mountains kept their shapes, rivers remembered their courses, and the stone under her feet remained stone.

A tear meant failure. Failure at that scale meant consequences she couldn't assess standing on a seawall in a garrison town the empire had forgotten.

The brand burned steady. She counted stones. Forty-three between the corner tower and the fisher's dock. Same as yesterday. Same as the day before.

She walked the rest of the patrol and told no one.

---

By mid-morning she was repairing the garrison's back wall, replacing mortar that had cracked over winter. The work was simple. Scrape out the old material, mix the new, press it into place, smooth the joint. Repeat. She'd fixed half the garrison this way — walls that didn't strictly need fixing but gave her hands something to do and kept Tavik from asking questions she wouldn't answer.

The back wall faced the market district. From here she could see the square, the Listing Mast, the row of shops that opened when they felt like it and closed for no reason she'd ever determined. Duskhaven operated on its own time. She'd learned not to expect logic.

She was smoothing a joint when the awareness arrived: someone was watching.

Not casual watching. Not a passerby glancing at the woman working mortar on a cool spring morning. This was assessment. The weight of it settled between her shoulder blades like a hand she couldn't see.

Kaelith didn't turn. She finished the joint, set down her trowel, and reached for the bucket. Slow. Deliberate. Giving herself time to think.

She'd spent six years in the Ashborne Legion before Calwrath. Six years learning to read a battlefield, to know when someone had acquired her as a target before they pulled a weapon. That skill hadn't eroded. If anything, exile had stripped it to load-bearing essentials — she noticed threats now because she'd stopped pretending they weren't there.

She turned.

A woman stood at the corner of the market square, thirty feet away. Fae, or close enough that the difference didn't matter. Pale skin with an undertone that wasn't quite human. Black hair pinned up. Grey-blue eyes that didn't look away when Kaelith met them.

Her palms went warm. The brand pulsed — a single sharp beat, then settled.

That was the tell. Humans looked away when caught staring. This woman held the gaze, steady and deliberate and aimed.

Kaelith read the rest in seconds. Lean build, precise posture, clothing that was good quality but unremarkable — the kind of unremarkable that required attention. Traveler, maybe. Merchant liaison, if she didn't look close. But merchants didn't hold a position and sight a target from it. Merchants didn't stand like that.

The woman's hands were empty. Visible. That was intentional too. No weapon drawn, no threat offered — just presence, and the discipline to hold it.

Kaelith noticed.

She held the woman's gaze for three more seconds — long enough to acknowledge, not long enough to challenge — then turned back to the wall. She picked up her trowel. Scraped mortar. Pressed it into the joint. Her hands were steady. The brand burned.

Behind her, footsteps. Not approaching. Moving parallel, staying at distance. Watching.

Kaelith worked mortar until the footsteps faded into the market noise, then she stopped. She set down the trowel and looked at her hands. No tremor. Good. She was fine.

She counted the joints she'd finished. Seventeen. She needed twenty to complete this section. Three more, then she could stop.

She worked the last three joints and left the bucket where it was.

---

Tavik was in his office when she found him, hunched over a ledger that probably didn't matter. Duskhaven's garrison commander in name only — the title came with a building and a stipend and the understanding that nothing he did would ever matter to anyone in Aranthem. He looked up when she entered, and his shoulders dropped a fraction — not startled, but settled, like a man who'd been waiting for a knock he'd hoped wouldn't come.

"Kaelith."

"There's a woman in the market. Fae, or close enough. Watching."

Tavik set down his pen. His jaw tightened — a small motion, quickly suppressed. "A merchant liaison. Arrived two days ago."

"Liaison for what? We don't trade enough to warrant oversight."

"The Cerith Court maintains connections across the border provinces." He said it the way soldiers recite orders they didn't write. Almost naturally.

Kaelith leaned against the doorframe. Kept her voice level. "She's watching me."

"She's probably just—"

"She's not *probably* anything." Kaelith straightened. "She stood at the corner of the square for ten minutes. She didn't move. She didn't look at anyone else. When I looked back, she didn't flinch."

Tavik's hands flattened on the desk. "I don't know what you want me to do about it."

"Tell me who she is. Actually. Not the line someone fed you."

"I told you—"

"You're lying." Flat. A fact of engineering, not an accusation.

He looked at her for a long moment. Something moved through his expression — not anger, but something older and quieter. The tension of a man who has learned when to stop talking. He picked up his pen again. "I can't help you, Kaelith."

"Can't, or won't?"

"Does it matter?"

She studied him. Tavik had been garrison commander for eight years. He'd been here when she arrived, and he'd never pressed her for details about Calwrath, never treated her like anything other than a useful exile who fixed walls and didn't cause problems. She'd thought that was decency. Now she reconsidered.

Decency, or orders?

"It matters," she said.

He didn't look up. "I'm sorry."

Kaelith turned and left. She walked through the garrison courtyard, past the empty barracks where the Ashborne Legion's standard still hung from a pole no one had bothered to remove. The brand burned steady. She didn't touch it.

She walked back to the room she rented above the Listing Mast and bolted the door. Then she sat on the edge of the bed and looked at her hands.

They were shaking now.

She made herself count. Three exits from this room: door, window, the loose board in the floor that led to the tavern's storage cellar. She'd memorized them her first night here. Force of habit. Force of the training that had survived Calwrath.

The fae woman was here for her. That much was obvious. Why was less clear. Kaelith had been erased — no rank, no authority, no threat to anyone. She fixed walls in a garrison town no one remembered. She was *nothing.*

Except the brand hadn't dissolved. Someone was maintaining it. That took Conclave-level Shapers, the knowledge to refresh disciplinary wards, and the intent to keep her contained.

And now someone was watching.

She pressed her palm to her collarbone. The brand flared, sharp and immediate, like it always did when her heart rate climbed. Pain was information. The information was: you're not safe. You've never been safe. You just stopped noticing.

Kaelith lay back on the bed and stared at the ceiling. The wood was stained dark from years of damp. Someone had carved initials into the beam above the window — *J.T. YK 730* — but whoever they were, they hadn't stayed.

She closed her eyes.

The Pallor had torn. The brand had flared. A predator had arrived and made herself visible. Tavik was compromised.

These were facts. Kaelith knew how to work with facts. She assessed the threat, calculated resources, and made a decision. That was command. That was survival.

Her resources: none. Her options: fewer.

The smart move was to leave Duskhaven. Pack what little she owned, walk out at dawn, and disappear into one of the border provinces where the Pallor was thinner and the empire's reach didn't extend. She'd thought about it before. The only thing that had kept her here was inertia — the dull weight of believing it didn't matter where she rotted.

But staying here meant staying visible. Staying visible meant staying a target.

And leaving meant admitting that six years of penance hadn't bought her the right to stop running.

Kaelith opened her eyes. The ceiling hadn't changed. The brand still burned. Outside, the fog sat thick against the glass, dense enough to swallow the square.

She sat up. She didn't know who the fae woman was, or what Tavik had been told, or why six years of quiet decay had suddenly produced a watcher.

But she knew this: the empire didn't forget people by accident. People got forgotten because someone wanted them forgotten. And when someone started watching, it meant they'd stopped wanting silence and started wanting something worse.

Plaster dust and pine resin — there and gone before she could flinch.

The smart move was to leave.

Kaelith had spent six years making the smart move. Six years of fixing walls and keeping her head down and pretending that if she atoned hard enough, the burning would stop.

The burning hadn't stopped.

She stood. Crossed to the window. Looked down at the market square.

The fae woman was gone. The square held only a fishmonger closing his stall and children throwing stones at a seabird on the fountain's edge. Normal. Unremarkable.

She watched until the square emptied, then turned from the window.

She owned: a belt knife kept sharp from habit, two changes of clothes, and nothing else that would last a day on the road. No armor. No weapons worth the name. No coin beyond what Tavik paid her for repair work — never enough to leave.

She'd been stripped of everything at Calwrath. Everything that made her dangerous.

Except they'd missed one thing.

She sat on the bed and placed her palm flat against her sternum — not the brand above her collarbone, but lower, deeper, where something answered. Faint. Distant. Like a sound from the other side of a wall so thick she couldn't tell if it was asking for help or warning her to run.

Magic.

The Oathbrand suppressed it. Locked it behind a barrier she couldn't cross or break. But suppression wasn't removal. She could feel the capacity sometimes when the brand flared — a pressure building, a heat that had nowhere to go.

If she could get the brand removed, the magic would return.

If she could get the magic back, she could leave Duskhaven and mean it. She could disappear into the provinces where the Conclave's reach thinned, where no one cared what she'd done or who she'd been.

If. If. If.

Kaelith lowered her hand. The brand settled into its rhythm: a steady burn she'd learned to live with, the way she'd learned to live with everything else. It was fine. She was fine.

She lay back down and listened to the wind rattle the shutters.

Somewhere in Duskhaven, a fae woman was watching. Tavik was lying. The Pallor was tearing. And Kaelith had no resources, no plan, and no reason to believe tomorrow would be different from today.

She closed her eyes and counted stones until she stopped shaking.

The seawall held. It always held.

She just wasn't sure how long she would.
