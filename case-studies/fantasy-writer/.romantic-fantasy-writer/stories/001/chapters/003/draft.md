# Chapter Three

Tavik's office was unlocked. That was the first problem.

Kaelith stood in the garrison's administrative corridor an hour past midnight, one hand on the doorframe, calculating. Tavik locked his office. Every night. She'd watched him do it for five years — routine as tide, predictable as stone. The man ran Duskhaven's garrison on procedure and habit because thinking too hard about his irrelevance would break something in him that still needed to believe military service mattered.

But tonight the door sat open two inches. Light spilled through the gap, thin and yellow, the kind of illumination that came from a single candle burning low.

Kaelith pushed the door wider. Slowly. The hinges were oiled; Tavik maintained them himself, one of the small disciplines he clung to. The door swung silent.

The office was empty. The candle burned on Tavik's desk, wax pooling around the base, flame guttering in the draft from the window he always left cracked because he said the sea air helped him think. His chair sat pushed back, angled wrong, as if he'd left in a hurry.

On the desk: three sealed letters, a half-finished report, and a leather dispatch case she recognized from High Conclave courier protocols. The case sat open, buckles undone, flap thrown back like someone had been interrupted mid-packing.

Kaelith stepped inside. Closed the door behind her. Listened.

The garrison was quiet. Down the hall, someone snored — Merik, the quartermaster, who drank himself unconscious most nights and woke with self-loathing he tried to drown again the next evening. Outside, wind off the water rattled loose shutters. Duskhaven at night sounded like a place slowly forgetting it was inhabited.

She moved to the desk. Didn't touch anything yet. Just looked.

The letters were addressed in Tavik's careful script: two to administrative contacts in regional garrison offices, routine reports that wouldn't matter to anyone. The third was addressed to someone in Aranthem, no department listed, just a building number in the Conclave's administrative quarter. That one had a wax seal already pressed: Tavik's garrison mark, a lighthouse over waves. Official correspondence.

The dispatch case held more. She could see the edge of folded papers, the kind of parchment the Conclave used for encrypted communications — heavier stock, treated with alchemical signature so they couldn't be forged or altered without detection. Tavik shouldn't have access to those. He ran a border garrison that processed fishing tariffs and logged ship movements. He had no business receiving High Conclave encrypted messages.

The Oathbrand flared. Heat spread from her collarbone in a sharp wave, sudden enough that her breath caught. She pressed her palm against it, felt the raised scar tissue through her shirt, the persistent burn that should have dissolved years ago.

The pain settled into its usual rhythm. She counted three breaths. Waited for her pulse to steady.

Then she reached for the dispatch case.

The encrypted messages were folded into thirds, sealed with red wax bearing marks she didn't recognize — not Conclave administrative seals but something older, more formal. The kind of mark that came with authority high enough that most people never saw it directly. She broke the first seal carefully, unfolded the parchment, angled it toward the candlelight.

The text was brief. Coded, but not heavily — whoever sent this assumed the recipient would understand context without elaborate encryption. She read it once. Twice. Her hands stayed steady.

> **MAREN, K. — STATUS INQUIRY**
> 
> Confirm subject remains at Duskhaven garrison. Confirm suppression remains effective — no reports of magical activity, no attempts at Oathbrand removal, no indication subject has regained operational capacity. Continue monitoring health status. If deterioration accelerates beyond projected parameters, report immediately. Maintenance schedule proceeds per previous instruction. Next refresh: 14 days.
> 
> No direct contact. Observation only.

Kaelith set the message down. Stared at it. Felt something cold and sharp settle in her chest like a blade finding space between ribs.

*Maintenance schedule.* 

The Oathbrand wasn't malfunctioning. It was being *refreshed*. Deliberately. On a schedule coordinated with someone who had enough authority to send encrypted dispatches to a border garrison commander and expect compliance.

The brand should have dissolved five years ago. She'd served her term, endured her punishment, accepted exile as the price of Calwrath. The burn should have faded. The suppression should have lifted. She should have been left alone to rot quietly in Duskhaven until she forgot she'd ever been anything other than a woman who fixed walls that didn't need fixing.

But someone had decided that wasn't sufficient. Someone wanted her suppressed. Monitored. Maintained in exile like a weapon stored but not destroyed, kept accessible in case they decided later that execution was cleaner than containment.

She read the second message. Same seal. Same coded brevity.

> **RE: MAREN INQUIRY**
> 
> Subject remains contained. Health status: declining but stable. Oathbrand suppression holding at expected efficacy. No indication of ontological activation. No attempts to access suppressed capacity. Subject presents as compliant with exile terms, no security concerns flagged.
> 
> Notable: Cerith Court liaison arrived Duskhaven 6 days prior. Surveillance confirmed. Purpose unclear. Recommend continued observation. Will report any changes.

Kaelith's jaw tightened. She forced it to relax. Forced herself to keep reading.

The third message was older — the wax seal more brittle, the parchment edges slightly yellowed. Dated eight months ago.

> **PREVENTION PROTOCOL — MAREN, K.**
> 
> Suppression must be maintained indefinitely. Subject must not regain access to ontological capacity. If Oathbrand shows signs of natural dissolution, refresh immediately using standard Shaper protocols. Authorization attached.
> 
> Rationale: Subject's Warden-lineage capacity represents unacceptable risk given current Pallor instability. Restoration would enable subject to interface with god-seal architecture. Access to Quietus location must remain inaccessible. Execution remains undesirable due to political complications re: Ashborne Legion loyalists. Containment via suppression is preferred strategy.
> 
> Costs approved. Maintain silence.

The candle flame wavered. The room felt smaller. Kaelith read the message again, slower, making sure she understood every word correctly.

*Warden-lineage capacity.*

*Access to Quietus location.*

*Execution remains undesirable.*

She set the message down carefully. Her hands were still steady. That felt wrong. Her hands should be shaking. She should be feeling something — rage, fear, betrayal, anything. But what she felt instead was the cold, clear focus that came when a tactical situation clarified from chaos into legible threat.

She knew what she was looking at. Not just exile. Not punishment for Calwrath. This was containment. Strategic suppression. Someone on the High Conclave believed she carried something they couldn't allow her to access, something valuable enough to keep her alive but dangerous enough to keep her caged. And they'd been doing it for five years without telling her what they thought she was.

*Warden-lineage.*

She didn't know what that meant. She knew the Wardens had been keepers of the Quietus, the god-seal that contained Ythara the Unfinished. She knew the Warden order had been dismantled during the Consolidation Wars seventy years ago, their knowledge declared too dangerous to preserve. She knew the High Conclave had spent decades erasing Warden history from official records because reality-manipulation at that scale made empires nervous.

But she didn't know why anyone would think *she* carried Warden inheritance. Her parents had been Legion officers. Her grandparents had been farmers in the midlands. There was no mystical lineage, no secret bloodline, nothing in her history that suggested she was anything other than a woman who'd been trained to command soldiers and had made one terrible decision at a siege five years ago that killed three hundred civilians.

Unless the terrible decision wasn't the point. Unless Calwrath had been the excuse to impose exile, and exile had been the mechanism to impose suppression, and suppression had been the strategy to prevent her from accessing something she didn't know she had.

Unless everything she'd believed about her disgrace was a lie designed to keep her contained while someone decided whether she was more useful alive and caged or dead and gone.

Kaelith memorized the messages. Word for word. She couldn't take them — Tavik would notice, and whoever was monitoring him would know she'd been here. But she could carry the information out in her head, and once it was there, they couldn't take it back.

She refolded the parchment exactly as she'd found it. Pressed the broken wax seals back into place as closely as she could manage. They wouldn't hold scrutiny, but if Tavik was frightened enough, he might not look too closely. He might assume he'd been careless, that the seals had cracked in transit or in storage.

She returned everything to the dispatch case. Positioned it exactly as it had been: buckles undone, flap thrown back, as if interrupted mid-packing. She adjusted the chair to match the angle she'd found it.

Then she moved to the door. Listened. The garrison remained quiet.

She stepped into the corridor. Closed the door softly, leaving it open the same two inches she'd found it. Walked the length of the hall with measured steps, nothing hurried, nothing furtive. Just a woman who couldn't sleep walking the garrison because insomnia was routine and no one cared what you did past midnight in a place like Duskhaven.

She reached her quarters. Closed the door. Locked it. Sat on the edge of her narrow bed and stared at her hands.

They were shaking now.

---

The enforcer arrived at dawn.

Kaelith saw him from her window — tall figure in dark grey travel leathers crossing the market square with two armed subordinates flanking him. He moved like someone who expected obstacles to remove themselves. The subordinates moved like people who'd learned to be the removal mechanism.

She counted their weapons. Longswords, standard Legion issue. Belt knives. One carried a crossbow strapped across his back. The enforcer himself wore no visible weapons, which meant either he was overconfident or his weapons were the kind you didn't advertise. Conclave operatives carried binding sigils, suppression wards, paralytic charms — tools designed for capture rather than combat. Tools that didn't care whether you fought back.

Kaelith stepped away from the window. Dressed quickly: sturdy trousers, reinforced tunic, boots she could run in if running became necessary. She strapped her belt knife where she could reach it. Not that a knife would matter against three Conclave-trained operatives, but going unarmed felt like surrender, and she wasn't ready to surrender yet.

She left her quarters. Took the back stairs down to the garrison's main level. Moved toward the courtyard.

In the courtyard, Tavik was already speaking with the enforcer. From thirty feet away, Kaelith could read the garrison commander's posture: shoulders drawn in, hands held carefully still, gaze fixed on the enforcer's chest rather than his face. Fear. Not the reactive fear of immediate threat but the chronic fear of someone who'd learned that certain people could destroy you with administrative precision and there was nothing you could do to stop them.

The enforcer was speaking. Tavik was nodding. One of the subordinates glanced around the courtyard, cataloguing exits, threat vectors, positions of concealment. Professional. Cold. The kind of assessment Kaelith had done herself a thousand times before Calwrath.

She stepped into the courtyard.

The enforcer's attention shifted immediately. Locked onto her with the focus of someone who'd been told exactly what to look for and had just found it. He said something to Tavik — brief, dismissive — then turned fully toward Kaelith.

"Kaelith Maren."

Not a question. Confirmation. She stopped ten feet from him. Close enough to hear clearly. Far enough that his subordinates would have to move to reach her.

"Who's asking?"

"Enforcer Seldric, High Conclave administrative authority." He produced a folded document from inside his coat, held it up without offering it to her. "I have orders for your immediate transfer to Aranthem for medical evaluation of your Oathbrand status."

Kaelith didn't look at the document. Kept her eyes on Seldric's face. He was maybe forty, clean-shaven, with the kind of controlled expression that came from years of delivering bad news and not caring how people received it.

"The Oathbrand was evaluated five years ago," she said. "Standard disciplinary term. One year suppression. Term completed."

"The evaluation is to determine why the brand persists beyond expected parameters." Seldric's tone was flat. Procedural. As if this were routine administrative work and her cooperation was assumed. "You'll accompany us to Aranthem. Travel will take four days. Accommodations have been arranged."

"I don't require medical evaluation."

"The evaluation is mandatory."

"On whose authority?"

Seldric's expression didn't change. "High Conclave authority. The orders are signed and sealed. You can read them in transit."

Kaelith counted her options. They were few. She could refuse — but refusal meant they'd take her anyway, and resisting Conclave authority in front of witnesses gave them legal justification to escalate suppression protocols. She could comply — but compliance meant getting into a transport with three Conclave operatives heading toward the capital, where medical evaluation was bureaucratic euphemism for rendition, interrogation, or disappearance depending on what they decided she knew.

She could run. But running meant leaving Duskhaven with no resources, no destination, no plan beyond immediate survival. And the enforcer's subordinates were already positioned to cut off the courtyard's two primary exits.

The Oathbrand burned steady. A low heat that sharpened when she thought about using magic she didn't have access to.

"I need time to gather my things," she said.

Seldric nodded. "You have ten minutes. My men will accompany you."

"That's not necessary."

"It's procedure." He gestured to one of the subordinates — the one with the crossbow. "Ensure she has what she needs. Nothing more."

The subordinate moved forward. Kaelith held her ground. Let him come within arm's reach. He was younger than Seldric, maybe mid-thirties, with the blank professionalism of someone who followed orders without processing their implications.

"Move," he said quietly.

Kaelith turned. Walked toward the garrison building. The subordinate followed three paces behind — close enough to intervene if she did something unpredictable, far enough that it didn't look like escort under duress. In the courtyard behind her, Seldric was speaking to Tavik again. She couldn't hear the words, but she could guess: cooperation, documentation, official reports. The procedural machinery that made disappearance look like administrative process.

She reached the building's entrance. The subordinate stayed on her heels.

Then: a sound behind them. Sharp and sudden — the clatter of something heavy falling, wood striking stone, the specific crash of scaffolding collapsing. Kaelith turned.

In the courtyard, one of the merchant stalls had toppled. The wooden frame lay across the cobblestones, canvas awning twisted around the support poles, goods spilling everywhere. The merchant was shouting. Seldric's other subordinate had been standing near the stall — now he was on the ground, tangled in canvas, swearing as he tried to free himself.

Seldric moved toward the fallen stall. Annoyed. The subordinate behind Kaelith hesitated, looking back toward his superior for instruction.

Kaelith saw Lirien.

The fae woman stood at the edge of the market square, thirty feet from the collapsed stall, hands empty and visible. She wasn't looking at the chaos. She was looking at Kaelith. And her expression was absolutely neutral — the kind of neutral that came from deliberately showing nothing while communicating everything.

*I did that. This is a window. Use it.*

The subordinate behind Kaelith made a decision. "Stay here," he said, and moved toward the courtyard to help his partner.

Kaelith stayed. For exactly three seconds. Long enough that disobedience wouldn't be immediate, short enough that opportunity didn't close.

Then she turned and walked — not ran, *walked* — toward the garrison's side corridor. The one that led to the back entrance. The one the subordinate had failed to block when he'd chosen to help his partner instead of maintaining prisoner security.

She reached the corridor. Kept walking. Behind her: shouting, the sound of canvas ripping, Seldric's voice raised in command. Ahead: the back door, closed but unlocked because Duskhaven's garrison didn't expect internal security breaches.

She pushed through the door. Stepped into the alley behind the garrison. Looked left: dead end. Looked right: narrow passage leading toward the market district's outer edges.

Lirien appeared at the alley's mouth. Not running. Walking with the same deliberate calm Kaelith had used. As if two women meeting in an alley at dawn were perfectly normal and no one needed to pay attention.

They looked at each other.

"You should go," Lirien said quietly.

"Why did you do that?"

"The stall? Poor construction. Unsafe scaffolding. Happens frequently in border towns."

"Why did you do that," Kaelith repeated. Not asking. Demanding.

Lirien's expression stayed neutral. But something shifted in her eyes — calculation resolving into decision, the moment when an operative stopped playing the part and admitted what they were actually doing.

"Because I need you alive," Lirien said. "And they were going to take you somewhere you wouldn't come back from."

Behind them, footsteps in the garrison corridor. Someone had noticed her absence. The window was closing.

"I don't trust you," Kaelith said.

"You shouldn't." Lirien stepped back, opening space. "But you don't have better options. And neither do I."

The footsteps grew louder. A voice shouting — the subordinate realizing his prisoner had walked out the back door while he was distracted by falling merchandise.

Kaelith made the calculation. Trust no one. Survive alone. Die clean.

Or trust the enemy who just acted like an ally and see what that bought her.

She moved. Toward Lirien. Toward the alley's mouth and the market district beyond and whatever temporary safety could be found in a town where everyone was watching and no one would intervene because intervention meant getting involved in Conclave business, which was the fastest way to become a problem that needed solving.

Lirien turned and walked beside her. Not ahead, not behind. Parallel. Two women crossing the market square in early-morning half-light, moving with purpose but not panic, nothing in their body language suggesting flight.

Behind them: the garrison. The enforcer. The subordinates who'd failed to maintain custody. The transport that would have taken her to Aranthem and whatever lay beyond that polite bureaucratic word *evaluation*.

Ahead: Duskhaven's salt-stained streets. The market. The sea. And the woman who'd just disrupted a Conclave capture operation without being asked, for reasons Kaelith didn't understand and couldn't trust but had just chosen to rely on anyway.

They reached the market's far edge. Lirien turned down a side street, narrow and shadowed, leading away from the main square.

Kaelith followed.

The Oathbrand burned. The Pallor pressed down overhead like tarnished silver weight. And somewhere behind them, the enforcer was realizing his prisoner had just walked away with assistance from someone who shouldn't have been there.

The categories were breaking. Enemy. Ally. Threat. Protection. Five minutes ago Kaelith had known what Lirien was. Now she didn't.

Now she just knew they were moving in the same direction, away from the same threat, and that was going to have to be enough.

---

They stopped in an abandoned warehouse three streets from the market. Kaelith checked the space systematically: two exits, one window high enough that climbing out would be slow, no active concealment but enough shadow and stacked crates that they could hide if someone searched casually. Not safe. Temporarily unobserved.

Lirien stood near the far wall, watching her. Not crowding. Giving space.

"How long before they find us?" Kaelith asked.

"An hour if they're thorough. Less if they're motivated."

"They're motivated."

"Then we don't stay here." Lirien moved to the window, glanced out, then back at Kaelith. "You need to leave Duskhaven. Today. They'll lock down the port and search every building once Seldric reports your disappearance to Aranthem."

Kaelith leaned against a crate. Let herself feel the exhaustion for three seconds — just long enough to acknowledge it existed, not long enough to let it undermine function. "Why did you help me?"

"I told you. I need you alive."

"Why?"

Lirien was quiet for a moment. Calculating. Deciding how much truth was tactically viable. "Because you're more complicated than my orders accounted for. And I don't execute people when the orders don't make sense."

"Your orders." Kaelith watched Lirien's face. Read the controlled expression, the deliberate neutrality that was starting to feel like its own kind of honesty. "The Cerith Court liaison. That's what Tavik called you. But you're not a merchant liaison."

"No."

"You're an operative."

"Yes."

"Sent to Duskhaven for me."

Lirien didn't flinch. Didn't deflect. Just held Kaelith's gaze and said, "Yes."

Kaelith felt the cold blade-sharp clarity settle deeper. "To kill me."

"To assess you." Lirien's voice stayed level. "Execution was one possible outcome. It's no longer the outcome I'm pursuing."

"Why not?"

"Because the people who sent the enforcer want you disappeared. And the people who sent me want information about why. And when two groups want incompatible things from the same target, it means the target is more valuable than either group is admitting." Lirien stepped away from the window. "You read Tavik's dispatches last night. I saw you leave his office."

Kaelith's hand moved toward her knife. Stopped. The motion would mean nothing against an operative who'd been watching her that closely. "You were following me."

"I've been following you for six days. Last night you did something different. You broke into a secure office and read classified correspondence. That's not exile behavior. That's operational behavior. And it means you know something now that you didn't know yesterday."

"You want to know what I found."

"I want to know what you are." Lirien's tone stayed even. "The High Conclave thinks you're dangerous enough to keep suppressed indefinitely. The Cerith Court thinks you're valuable enough to warrant a contract they don't typically accept. And you just walked away from a Conclave enforcer with my help, which makes us both targets now. So yes, I want to know what you are. Because I need to know whether keeping you alive is strategy or suicide."

Kaelith counted her options again. They'd narrowed. She could kill Lirien — try to, at least, though the operative's positioning suggested she'd anticipated that possibility and prepared for it. She could run — but running without resources in a town about to be locked down was slow death. She could cooperate — share what she'd learned, accept temporary alliance with someone who'd just admitted to being sent to kill her, and see whether mutual need was enough to build trust on.

The categories were already broken. Might as well see how far they'd shatter.

"The Oathbrand is being maintained deliberately," Kaelith said. "Someone on the High Conclave is keeping me suppressed because they think I have something they call Warden-lineage capacity. They think I can access something called the Quietus. And they're afraid of what happens if I do."

Lirien went very still. The kind of stillness that came from information landing with implications that required immediate recalculation.

"Warden lineage," she said quietly. "You're sure?"

"That's what the messages said. Prevention protocol. Suppression must be maintained indefinitely. Subject must not regain access to ontological capacity." Kaelith pushed off the crate. "I don't know what it means. I don't know why they think I have it. But I know they're willing to lie about exile, maintain suppression past legal term, and send an enforcer to disappear me before I figure out what they're hiding. So either they're wrong about what I am, or they're right and I've been walking around not knowing I'm carrying something that scares the High Conclave."

"If you have Warden capacity," Lirien said slowly, "and the Pallor is failing — which it is — then you're not just a political problem. You're a strategic asset. And assets that valuable don't get left unsecured." She looked at Kaelith with something that might have been respect or might have been professional reassessment. "The enforcer wasn't here for medical evaluation. He was here to disappear you before anyone else figures out what you might be able to do."

"Can I? Do anything?"

"I don't know." Lirien moved toward the door. Checked the street beyond. "But if the Conclave believes you can, then we need to leave Duskhaven before they bring enough resources to make leaving impossible. And we need to find out whether they're right. Because if they are, you're the only person who might be able to access the god-seal. And if the Pallor is tearing—"

"Then Ythara wakes up," Kaelith finished. "And reality stops being finished."

They looked at each other. Two operatives. Two targets. One conspiracy neither of them had chosen.

The parameters were clear. The outcome was not.

But they were moving in the same direction now. Away from the enforcer. Toward answers. Into whatever came next.

"There's a smuggler's boat leaving the port in two hours," Lirien said. "I can get us passage if you can be ready to leave everything behind."

Kaelith thought about her quarters. The narrow bed. The tools she used to fix walls that didn't need fixing. Five years of exile condensed into a room she wouldn't miss.

"I'm ready," she said.

Lirien nodded. Opened the door. Stepped into the street.

Kaelith followed.

Behind them: Duskhaven. The garrison. The enforcer who'd tried to take her. The life that had never been anything but a cage she'd pretended was choice.

Ahead: the port. A smuggler's boat. The woman who'd been sent to kill her and had chosen to keep her alive instead.

The Oathbrand burned steady. The Pallor pressed down. And Kaelith walked toward the water with an enemy-turned-ally beside her, trusting nothing except the fact that they both needed each other more than they needed anything else.

It would have to be enough.
