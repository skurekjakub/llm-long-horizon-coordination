# Chapter Fourteen

Dawn was thin and grey, the Pallor's membrane catching the ridgeline light and bending it silver. Lirien sat with her back against the ledge's granite lip, counting Kaelith's breaths. Not deliberately — the bond did it for her, each exhale arriving at her awareness like a second pulse. Kaelith was three paces ahead, one hand trailing the rock face where camp met slope, her silhouette sharp against the pale sky.

Quiet. The kind of quiet that pressed against the inner ear, fragile as glass and about as trustworthy.

Three people in the treeline. Two behind granite, one elevated.

Lirien registered the configuration before the first suppression field crackled to life — a sudden flattening of air, as if someone had pressed a wet cloth against her inner ear. Kaelith's attention shifted. Through the bond: not words, not thoughts. The quality of her awareness compressing — from the loose warmth that had carried them through the predawn hours to something bright and very, very still.

*She felt it too.*

"Down," Lirien said, already moving.

The first bolt of shaped force struck the granite where Kaelith's head had been. Stone fragments peppered the air — chips no larger than fingernails, but fast enough to cut. Lirien catalogued: military-grade shaping, tight focal point, the kind of precision that required trained practitioners, not conscripted thugs. Three suppression anchors humming at the edges of her perception, triangulated to create a dead zone where fae resonance would stutter and fail.

Designed for her. Specifically.

Which meant Thalen's people had studied what she could do.

A second strike came from the elevated position — west, behind a stand of twisted pine that had grown sideways under the Pallor's weight. Lirien rolled behind the ledge's natural lip, her back against cold stone, and pulled the count. Three attackers visible. The suppression field suggested a fourth somewhere holding the anchors. That one wouldn't fight; they'd maintain the field, which meant they were the priority.

The problem was the separation geometry. Two positions flanked the granite shelf, one on each side of the slope. The third held the high ground. It was a net designed to funnel them apart — Kaelith toward the slope where the terrain favored kinetic engagement, Lirien into the open where the suppression field would cripple her.

Classic isolation protocol. She'd designed configurations like this herself. Twice, at least. The geometry was familiar.

Kaelith's heartbeat arrived at her sternum — elevated, steady. A pressure there that she had learned to read as concentration, a tightening, like a seal finding its pressure point.

Kaelith moved.

Toward Lirien's position, not the slope. Closing the gap the ambush was designed to open. The intent arrived as sensation: a directional pull, Kaelith's awareness angling toward Lirien the way a current redirects when it finds the gap in the rock.

A third bolt struck the ground between them. Dirt and gravel erupted. Lirien tasted copper from a split lip she hadn't felt open. Through the suppression field, her ability to shape was dampened to perhaps forty percent — enough for precision work, not enough for anything broad. Every manipulation would cost twice the effort and carry half the force.

So she would be precise.

The first attacker broke cover on the left flank — a woman in hunter's leathers with a blade that shimmered with residual shaping. Lirien tracked the approach: fast, low, using the terrain breaks for cover, angling to intercept Kaelith before she could reach the shelf. Tactically sound. The hunter was good.

But Lirien could feel Kaelith moving. She felt it before she saw it — the bond carrying Kaelith's physical state like a second heartbeat: the rhythm of her breathing, the tension in her legs as she shifted her weight, the sharp bright intention of a woman about to change direction.

Kaelith pivoted right.

Lirien struck left.

A thin blade of shaped force — surgical, everything she could push through the suppression field — caught the approaching hunter across the thigh. A disruption, across the muscle. The woman stumbled, her charge broken, and in the fraction of breath where her guard dropped, Kaelith was there. Two hits. Economical, devastating. The hunter folded.

Kaelith's satisfaction arrived brief and fierce. And beneath it, the humming awareness that Lirien had moved before the opening existed — had *created* the opening — because the bond told her where Kaelith was going to be before Kaelith arrived.

The second attacker came from the right, and this one was better.

Taller, heavier, with a shaped barrier that deflected Lirien's first probe like stone turning a blade. Male, mid-forties, moving with the particular economy of someone who had been doing violence for most of his life. His attack pattern was designed to overwhelm — rapid strikes interspersed with barrier pulses that forced Lirien to choose between offense and defense.

She chose offense. Kaelith would handle defense.

The knowledge arrived without deliberation. Plans required communication, agreed parameters, acknowledged roles. This was something the bond had built between them — a fluency that bypassed language, that lived in the body the way Kaelith's kinetic memory of the Quietus path lived in her bones. Lirien attacked because she knew, with the certainty of a shared heartbeat, that Kaelith was already shifting to cover the gap.

The bond transmitted impact.

A sensation — sudden, blooming, across Lirien's left ribs. She gasped. The pain was phantom but absolute: Kaelith had taken a hit, somewhere on her left side, and the bond delivered it as a body-wide flare that compressed the world to a bright, flat plane where only the pain and the granite ledge existed.

*Get up. She's still fighting. Move.*

Lirien shoved through the pain and shaped. Three rapid manipulations, each one costing her like drawing thread through wet sand. The first sealed the ground beneath the second attacker's forward foot — a thin crust of hardened earth that caught his boot and stalled his momentum for one beat. The second was a distraction, a flicker of reflected light in his peripheral vision. The third was the finishing stroke: a thread of force through the gap in his shaped barrier, precisely where the construction was weakest, aimed at the shoulder joint of his sword arm.

He screamed. His barrier collapsed. Kaelith finished him.

The third attacker — the one on the high ground — was already retreating. Lirien tracked the movement: scrambling through the twisted pines, heading east toward what would be an extraction point. This one had been the coordinator, the one who held the tactical picture and adjusted the others' positions. Letting them run meant a report reaching Thalen within days.

The choice took no time.

"The field anchor," Lirien said. Her voice came out steady, which was a lie the rest of her body contradicted — her hands were shaking, adrenaline turning her fingers into unreliable instruments. "Northeast, forty paces. They drop the field, I can reach the runner."

Kaelith shifted direction. Lirien felt her go — the specific displacement of Kaelith's presence in the bond's topology, like watching a lamp carried through a dark house.

Thirty seconds. The suppression field stuttered, then collapsed.

Lirien's full capability flooded back — a rush that felt like an inner ear clearing, the world snapping into full resolution, louder and more detailed and too bright. She shaped distance. The runner was eighty paces out, moving fast, and she threaded a binding through the trees that caught their ankle and sent them sprawling in the pine needles.

They did not get up.

In the sudden quiet, Lirien stood among the aftermath and catalogued. Four attackers. One unconscious, one injured and disarmed, one bound, one — the field anchor operator — whom Kaelith had dealt with through means Lirien felt but did not see. Kaelith's breathing, harsh and ragged. The left-side pain, still there but dulling. And beneath everything, something Lirien could not name with any term from her vocabulary — a feeling that had no classification because no one had built a classification for it.

They had won. And her hands were shaking. Not from the fight. From what the fight had proven.

They had won through the bond — through the impossible synchronization it created, the shared awareness that let two people fight as a single weapon. Every opening she'd created had been possible because she'd known where Kaelith would be. Every strike Kaelith had landed had been enabled by Lirien's disruption of the target's defenses.

Remove either of them, and the other would have died.

Through the bond, Kaelith's rib pain pulsed — dulling now but persistent, a slow heat that Lirien carried in her own side like a debt. She pressed one hand against the granite ledge and breathed.

Lirien's tactical mind performed the assessment with the cold clarity her training demanded: the bond was the most significant force multiplier she had ever encountered, and it functioned only through mutual vulnerability. Sever the bond — emotionally withdraw, betray the connection, break the trust that allowed it — and their combat effectiveness would collapse.

She had just staked her survival on a woman she was contractually bound to end.

Lirien found Kaelith by the field anchor's position — a shallow depression between two boulders where someone had set three resonance stones in a precise triangle. She had checked bindings on the three prisoners on her way here — wrists secured, a sleep-working layered over the unconscious pair. They would keep.

Kaelith was sitting against the larger boulder, breathing controlled, one hand pressed against her left ribs.

People first.

Kaelith's face: drawn tight around the mouth, but the jaw wasn't locked. Pain, not crisis. Color adequate under the freckles, which was good — blood loss showed in freckled skin before it showed anywhere else. Hands steady despite the rib hold. Grey-green eyes tracking Lirien's approach with focused attention.

Days of shared sensation — that was all it had been, though it felt like longer. Days that had taught her the specific textures of Kaelith's body in distress: the sharp bright flare of impact, the slow burn of bruising, the grinding ache of bone under stress. She knew without touching that the pain was surface, not deep. Bruised tissue, maybe a cracked rib.

"Let me see." Lirien knelt. Her hands found Kaelith's side, lifting the fabric, and the gesture was clinical in execution if not in origin. Fingers mapping the ribs through skin. Third and fourth on the left, tender but intact — bruised, possibly hairline-cracked, not displaced.

"Two down, one bound, the runner's secured." Status report. Cover for the fact that her fingers were lingering on warm skin they had no reason to touch.

"The anchor operator?" Kaelith's voice was rougher than usual. Clipped.

"Unconscious. You?"

"Hit me with something shaped. Blunt force through the barrier." A pause. Kaelith's hand covered Lirien's where it rested on her ribs. "I felt you take the echo."

"I noticed."

Silence held between them. Lirien's body knew the difference now — this was not the trained silence she used to draw people out. This silence had weight. It pressed against the edges of language and found the seams.

Her hands were still shaking. Adrenaline. That was true, and it was not sufficient.

"Lirien."

Kaelith's voice, stripped down. Two syllables carrying more information than the bond transmitted — because the bond carried what you *felt*, not what you *chose*, and Kaelith was choosing to say her name with that particular quality of quiet.

"We should check the perimeter." The words came out clipped. Professional. A reflex so old it preceded memory. When the body wanted something the mind hadn't authorized, you defaulted to protocol.

"We should." Kaelith didn't move. Her thumb traced a single line across Lirien's knuckles. "In a moment."

The warmth that Lirien had spent days learning to recognize settled at the base of her throat. Not gratitude. Not desire, though desire lived inside it like a current beneath still water. Something that had no name in her lexicon because the lexicon had been designed by people who believed that naming a thing contained it.

This could not be contained.

Lirien looked at Kaelith. Truly looked — not assessment, but the specific act of seeing someone and letting yourself be changed by what you saw. Dawn light caught the angular planes of Kaelith's face, highlighting the dusting of freckles across her nose, the blunt line of hair against her jaw, the collarbone where the Oathbrand's ghost still ached. Lirien could feel it — that phantom itch, the absence of the brand that was its own form of presence.

"You're bleeding," Kaelith said. Her hand left the ribs and found Lirien's chin, turning her face. The split lip. Gentle fingers, rough calluses. "Here."

"Minor."

"I know."

She had been trained to dismantle the layers between what a person said and what they meant, to find the value in the space between surface and depth.

None of that training had prepared her for wanting to close the gap instead of analyzing it.

Her hand moved. To Kaelith's jaw, where the muscle clenched when she was in pain and she didn't want to show it. Lirien's fingers found the tension there and held it — didn't smooth it away, didn't try to fix it. Just held.

*You don't have to carry this quietly.*

"Inside," Kaelith said, and the word didn't match any logical continuation of the conversation, but Lirien understood it because the bond filled in what language left out. Inside meant: *come closer. I don't want the space between us.*

The perimeter could wait.

The perimeter would have to wait, because Lirien Dael — the Seep's forty-fourth contract, the woman who had never before formed the sentence *I want* — was making a choice. She was aware of the choice the way she was aware of all choices: as a branching point, one path leading back toward the controlled surface she had maintained for her entire professional life, the other leading somewhere uncharted.

Risk assessment: catastrophic.

She kissed Kaelith anyway.

The second kiss. The first had been last night, tentative and devastating, a question asked with mouths. This was the answer. Lirien leaned into Kaelith carefully — mindful of the ribs, mindful of everything her body had learned about where Kaelith hurt — and kissed her with the deliberate precision she brought to everything, except that the precision crumbled almost immediately because Kaelith's mouth was warm and wind-roughened and Kaelith's hand was in her hair and the bond turned sensation back on itself: feeling herself being kissed through Kaelith's body, feeling Kaelith being kissed through her own, two signals overlapping until the boundary between *her* pleasure and *mine* dissolved like sugar in warm water.

Kaelith made a sound against her mouth — not pain, though the ribs must have protested. Something lower. Permission, maybe. Or surrender. Kaelith Maren, who had held walls and counted dead and carried six years of penance in her bones, letting a sound like that escape — it registered as an event Lirien had no classification for.

Lirien's fingers found the hem of Kaelith's shirt. Paused.

"Yes," Kaelith said. Barely a word. More breath than language.

Lirien pulled the fabric up and her mouth followed her hands, pressing against the bruised ribs with care that bordered on devotion. She felt Kaelith's breath catch — the specific sharpness of skin sensitized by adrenaline receiving a gentle mouth — and that transmission undid something in Lirien's chest that she had not known was locked.

*I'm not a home. I'm a weapon. I'm the contract that was designed to end you.*

The thought rose and the bond caught its shadow. Kaelith's hand stilled on Lirien's skin — a pause, barely a breath's width. A searching look, grey-green eyes finding Lirien's face. Lirien pressed the thought down, pressed it deep, and kissed the hollow of Kaelith's throat instead. After a moment, Kaelith's hand moved again.

They were in the open. The boulder provided partial cover. Four incapacitated attackers lay within a hundred-pace radius. This was the worst possible time and the worst possible place.

The counter-assessment, which her training had no framework for: they were alive because of each other, and the aliveness demanded acknowledgment, and words were insufficient, and the body was the only language either of them trusted enough to use.

She laid Kaelith down against the moss at the boulder's base with a care that she registered, distantly, as something she had never shown another living person. A care that existed only when the person underneath you mattered more than the mission above you.

*When did that happen?*

It didn't matter when. It had happened, and her hands were learning the topography of Kaelith's body — the dip of her waist, the sharp ridge of her hip, the hollow at the base of her throat where a heartbeat pulsed against Lirien's lips. She kissed that place and felt what it did to Kaelith: a cascade of warmth spreading from the contact point outward, like water through packed earth, dissolving tension in its path.

The Seep. Named for the thing that finds every crack, that goes where it is not invited, that cannot be stopped once it finds the way in. She was the water, and Kaelith was the structure, and the structure was opening to her, and she could not stop, did not want to stop, was terrified that she did not want to stop.

Kaelith's hands — rough, scarred, capable of holding walls — were careful on Lirien's skin. Unhurried. They found the places where Lirien's fae markers showed: the opalescent shimmer at the nape, the wrists where the skin thinned to translucence. Kaelith touched those places as if they were the most important parts of her, and Lirien felt what that touch meant — not curiosity about the otherness, but *recognition*. As if Kaelith's hands were saying: *this is you, this particular strangeness is yours, I want to know it.*

The specificity destroyed her.

She had experienced desire before. Professional encounters, managed and contained, the body's needs addressed with the same efficiency she brought to everything. But no one had ever touched the fae-marked places on her body and meant *this is you and I want all of it*. No one had ever transmitted, through a bond that could not lie, the particular quality of want that was also a kind of homecoming.

*Tell her*, something insisted. *Tell her what you carry. Tell her before she gives you anything else.*

She didn't say it. She pulled Kaelith closer. The bond made the closeness recursive — each touch echoing between them, building, until the boundary between *her* pleasure and *mine* became a question neither of them was asking. They were the same circuit. And the pleasure was something they were making together, in the space where two people briefly became one continuous thing.

Kaelith's mouth on her shoulder. The scrape of teeth against the clavicle. Lirien arched into it and the bond sent the arc back to Kaelith, who gasped, who pressed closer, who said Lirien's name like a word she'd been afraid to use and had decided to use anyway.

After, they breathed. Sweat cooling in the grey dawn. Moss damp against bare shoulders.

Lirien lay half across Kaelith's chest, careful of the ribs, her cheek against the collarbone where the Oathbrand's ghost lived. She could feel it — the phantom brand's residual hum, like touching a wall that remembered bearing weight it no longer carried. Beneath it: Kaelith's heartbeat, steady and real and *hers* in a way that made Lirien's throat close.

Neither apologized. Neither said *we shouldn't have*. Neither retreated into the professional distance that, an hour ago, might still have been possible.

Through the bond: Kaelith's state — a warmth so unfamiliar it registered as almost painful. Tentative. As if joy were a material Kaelith had never worked with and wasn't certain would hold.

Lirien's state — she couldn't name it. The system that named things had been overwhelmed, and what remained was simpler than any framework: this woman, this warmth, this body against hers, this specific rightness that had no justification and required none.

She had surrendered control completely. And it had felt right.

Her pulse should have spiked. It didn't. The calm itself was the warning.

"What happens after?"

Kaelith's voice was quiet, roughened by the morning and everything that had preceded it. She lay with one arm behind her head, the other wrapped loosely around Lirien's waist. Dawn had resolved into early morning — thin grey light filtering through the Pallor's membrane, giving the wilderness a quality of muted glass.

"After." Lirien let the word sit between them. *Do we still exist outside the emergency?*

"When this is done." Kaelith turned her head. Her eyes — grey-green, still carrying that bruised tenderness — found Lirien's. "When there's no immediate apocalypse requiring our attention. What do you want?"

The question was direct. It was so precisely Kaelith — a woman who built things, who needed to know the load-bearing specifications before she committed weight — that Lirien felt a flicker of warmth that had nothing to do with the body-heat between them.

"I want—" The word stuck. Lirien recognized the mechanism: her training, reaching up from somewhere deep and habitual, clamping down on the first-person declaration. *I want* was not an operative's sentence. Operatives assessed, recommended, executed. They did not *want*.

She started again. "There's a harbor in the southern Cerith territories. Vael Seren. The water comes in through a narrows and the current is deceptive — surface calm, undertow vicious. The fishing boats have to be built differently there. Heavier keels. The locals call them root-boats because they sit so deep."

Kaelith watched her. Waited. She was good at waiting — six years of exile had taught her the integrity of patience.

"I went there once, on an assignment. Between contracts. The light in the evening was — the harbor faces west, and the Pallor is thinner over water, and the sunset came through almost unfiltered. Real color. Not the silver." Lirien paused. Felt the edges of what she was building toward. "I stayed an extra day. I've never stayed an extra day."

"Vael Seren," Kaelith repeated, as if learning the architecture of it.

"It's not a plan. It's not even a hope. It's just — a harbor I stayed an extra day in, once. That's all I have."

"That's enough."

Silence again, but shaped differently now. Holding something.

"And you?" Lirien asked.

"I don't know." Kaelith's honesty bore weight. "I've spent six years not thinking past tomorrow's wall. Now there's someone I want tomorrows with, and I don't know what shape that takes."

*Someone I want tomorrows with.*

The words landed in Lirien's chest and stayed there, spreading outward, reaching the places she had kept sealed. She pressed her face into Kaelith's shoulder and breathed — not a sob. Just the need to have her face hidden for a moment while the impact registered.

"I can't promise—" She stopped herself. Recalibrated. The truth, or as much of it as she could offer without breaking everything. "There are... variables I haven't resolved. Things that complicate the trajectory."

"I know."

"You don't know what they are."

"No." Kaelith's arm tightened around her waist. "I know they exist. I know you're carrying something. The bond doesn't transmit details, but it transmits the weight." A pause. "I'm choosing not to demand the inventory."

Lirien's throat constricted. Kaelith had been taught — by six years of exile, by the thing that happened at Calwrath — that trust was the failure that preceded collapse. And she was choosing it anyway.

*I don't deserve this.*

*I am actively lying to you about the contract that was designed to stop your heart.*

The guilt arrived like tidewater — not crashing, not dramatic. Seeping. Rising through the cracks in the warmth, filling the spaces between *I want tomorrows with you* and *I was sent to ensure you have none*. Lirien held still and let it rise, because if she moved, if she spoke, the bond would transmit the specifics of what she felt, and Kaelith would know something was catastrophically wrong.

Breathe. Classify. Contain.

She could not classify this. The guilt was not situational. It was guilt about receiving another person's body, and heart, and cautious hope, while carrying inside her own blood the contractual mechanism of their destruction.

Forty-three completions. In thirty years of operations, Lirien had never felt guilt about a contract. Contracts were architecture — they defined the space, you moved within it, the building stood or fell on its own terms. You did not feel guilty about load calculations. You did not grieve for a wall that came down because the specifications required demolition.

But Kaelith was not a wall. And the contract was not architecture. And Lirien Dael, who had built her entire identity on the premise that obedience was loyalty and contracts were connection, was beginning to understand that the building she had lived inside her whole life had been a cage designed by someone who did not love her.

The Cerith Court had sent her here with Kaelith's blood in the binding. They had not told her about the Quietus. They had not told her about the Pallor sabotage. They had not told her that the woman she was contracted to end might be the only person capable of preventing an ontological catastrophe.

They had not cared.

Beneath her cheek, Kaelith's heartbeat held its steady count. The bond carried it with the fidelity of a shared pulse — each beat landing in Lirien's awareness a fraction after it landed in Kaelith's chest, close enough to track with her eyes closed.

The contract specified *cessation of heartbeat* as proof of execution. Lirien had read those words a thousand times. She had memorized the exact phrasing because operatives memorized their contracts the way soldiers memorized their orders — completely, precisely, so that compliance was automatic and doubt was architectural rather than personal.

*Cessation of heartbeat.*

A Seep principle surfaced, clear as a training instructor's voice: *The binding reads what it reads. Precision of language is precision of obligation.* She had used the principle before — twice in her career, exploiting the gap between what a contract *intended* and what its words *required*. The gap was narrow. But narrow was enough, if you knew where to cut.

Not death. Not permanent termination. Not destruction of consciousness or dissolution of identity.

*Cessation of heartbeat.*

Her fingers found the pulse point at Kaelith's wrist without deciding to. Two beats. Three. The rhythm was as familiar now as her own.

A heart could stop and restart. Lirien had seen it — field medicine, magical intervention, the precise application of force to cardiac rhythm. Seconds, not minutes. A pause in the mechanism, not an ending.

If Kaelith's heart stopped — truly stopped, for even a handful of seconds — the contract's literal terms would be satisfied. *Cessation* was a state, not a duration. The binding would read the heartbeat's absence as fulfillment. And if the heart restarted immediately after...

The binding would already have released.

But the loophole had always been there, buried in the contract's language. She had noticed it years ago — filed it as theoretical, too dangerous without a way to monitor the restart with absolute precision. What made it viable now — what transformed it from curiosity to executable plan — was the bond. Through the resonance, Lirien could feel Kaelith's heartbeat as clearly as her own, could monitor the cessation and restart with a fidelity no external instrument could match.

The bond that made them deadly in combat could save Kaelith's life by stopping it.

She pressed two fingers against her own pulse. Counted.

A loophole. The risk was absolute: stop a heart wrong, and it stayed stopped. The timing would need to be surgical. The intervention would need to be precise. And it would only work because the bond made the monitoring possible — because through the resonance, she could feel the exact moment of cessation and the exact moment of restart, the way she could feel Kaelith's breathing against her own ribs right now.

"You're quiet," Kaelith said.

"Thinking."

"About the variables?"

"About solutions." The grey territory between a lie and the truth, where operatives lived and where Lirien had always been most fluent.

Kaelith didn't press. Lirien felt the restraint — the deliberate choice not to probe, not to use the connection as an interrogation tool. Trust, offered freely, to someone who was using the silence to plan how to stop her heart.

*I will solve this. I will find the exact margin. I will stop your heart for three seconds and bring you back and the binding will release and we will go to Vael Seren and I will never tell you what it cost.*

Mission parameters, redefined. Save target from contract.

Lirien pressed her mouth to Kaelith's shoulder — a kiss that was also a seal, a promise made in the only language available to someone whose words were poisoned by omission.

"We should move," she said. "Thalen's network will send another team when this one doesn't report."

"Agreed." Kaelith sat up carefully, one hand bracing the bruised ribs. Winced. Didn't complain. "Northeast. The pull is stronger today."

The Quietus, calling through bone and kinetic memory. Kaelith's body knew the path the way water knew downhill — not through decision but through nature, through the deep grammar of what she was.

Lirien stood and began the process of reassembling herself. Checking weapons. Tying back hair. Scanning the treeline for movement. Returning, piece by piece, to the surface that the last hour had stripped away — the controlled expression, the posture that communicated competence rather than the wreckage of tenderness underneath.

But something had changed. The surface was the same, but what it rested on had shifted. New load paths. Weight distributed differently.

She was still carrying the contract. She was still carrying the lie.

But now she was also carrying the beginning of how to end it. And the woman walking beside her, ribs bruised and hands steady, did not know that the kiss on her shoulder had been a declaration of war — not against her, but against the terms of her death.

Lirien fell into step. Kaelith's presence in the bond was warm and constant and trusting, and every step northeast was a step toward the Quietus, and every step was a step Lirien took with the full weight of what she knew and what she hid and what she was planning to do.

The dawn was grey. The Pallor's membrane caught the light and bent it silver, and through the thin places, if you looked carefully, you could almost see the real sky — the one that existed before a god had flayed themselves to fix the world in place. The real sky was darker than the false one. Less kind.

But it was true. And Lirien was done with kind.

She walked northeast, beside the woman she would save, carrying the secret that would destroy them if she failed.
